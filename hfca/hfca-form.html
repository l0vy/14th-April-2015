Reach a consensus with stakeholders on what success looks like, and how available technologies can get you to success over the next two to four years.


Develop a shared vision focused on outcomes, not on technology.


Engage critical stakeholders: The teachers, principals, pastors, finance and IT personnel, families, and students. Engaging these stakeholders to develop and build consensus around a shared vision is crucial to long-term success.

Creating better outcomes with technology begins by understanding that it is a 2 to 4 year process.


Carefully planned implementation is critical to success: a full year of planning in advance of implementation is not uncommon.


Focus on the critical steps in planning: professional development, infrastructure, and curriculum.


Embrace planning and implementation as a continual process, and re-evaluate regularly.


"Teachers need to feel this is a process that they are leading and that will help them. Allow teachers to suggest applications and projects early on. It’s also critical to give them tools to learn with before deploying in the classroom."


"Provide teachers advance access to training and devices, at least 3-6 months before deployment. Professional development should be a continuous process, so plan and budget for sustained professional development. It’s the most important factor in achieving successful outcomes."


Engage tech-savvy teachers as advocates: successful programs use enthusiastic teacher advocates to whom less technology savvy teachers can turn to for quick help.


Principals lead change. Principals must take a lead in both the use of data to inform instruction and the movement from teacher-led to student-led instruction.


A thorough audit of existing network infrastructure should be made.


Plan new infrastructure to support high-density usage.


Infrastructure includes not only the bandwidth coming into the school from the internet provider, but internal Wi-Fi bandwidth, server storage space, routers and switches.


Initially plan on a minimum of 500 Kpbs of throughput to device per student, and by 2018, a full 2 Mbps.


Cabling in classrooms and public areas, need to provide appropriate access to the network for students and staff. Below are examples for installing these ”drops” for the average class size of 25 students per classroom. While a single access point per classroom can be adequate to address budget constraints, be sure to consider future needs. Pulling more than one cable into the classroom and public areas once is far more cost effective than repeating the process over time. For larger classrooms and public areas additional Ethernet drops are likely required depending on the number of people requiring access and size of the room.


Deploy a Mobile Device Management tool. A device independent management tool allows for upgrading, modifying, replacing, and locking devices and software across various platforms. Proper device management improves deployment, efficiency, enables scaling and is a critical component of a successful program.


Platforms have been identified and communicated to school personnel for the best means to hosting/sharing/storing content


Segregate access to student, teacher, administrative, and guest networks, which should all be on isolated network areas.


Plan for and develop a process for dealing with broken technology, cracked screens, lost passwords, and internet failure.


Implement a secure means of communication for students


Critically plan for backups. Both on-site and off-site backup is recommended.


"In many schools teachers or volunteers handle the networking technologies. While well-intentioned, volunteers’ skills may not be enough to manage a complex environment where professional expertise is required in upgrading, modifying, replacing, locking devices, device security, and backups. Engage professional resources to help manage complex technology. Where a school can’t afford a dedicated technology staff, engage an outside professional firm."


The cost of devices will be only 20-30% of your technology spend. The rest of the spend will be on professional development, bandwidth, access points, security, and other items critical to a successful deployment.


Identify funding sources and plan to budget for technology each year. This is not going to be a one-time cost. A long-term funding plan is a critical aspect of this project.


Especially important is funding for professional development for teachers. While it is tempting to reduce costs by cutting Professional Development, it is not advisable to attempt to save money this way: failure to embrace Professional Development will mean a failed program.

"Include both traditional and non-traditional measures of success. Traditional success measures include test scores, drop out rate, rates of discipline, attendance, college acceptance, college completion. Non-traditional success measures include performance on weekly and monthly assessments, student engagement, collaboration and communication."


"Technology is continually changing in both expected and unexpected ways. Expected changes over the next three to five years include. Digital content will move from traditional flat content to content which is more fully immersive. A dramatic increase in the density of high speed cellular date (LTE) networks may provide enough bandwidth to allow high density connectivity to the internet. More and more devices will come equipped with LTE chips allowing access to broadband cellular networks."
